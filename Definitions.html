Important Investing Terms and Definitions:

Bull:

Someone who believes a security will rise in the future.

Bear:

Someone who believes a security will fall in the future.

Short:

A position in which shares are borrowed and sold at current value in hopes of a downturn in the security to buy-back the shares and return them for a profit.

Long:

A position in which shares are bought at a certain price and held in hopes of the value of the security going up.

Sector:

The 11 distinct components of the stock market, all of which have their own industries/sub-sectors. The 11 primary sectors are consumer discretionary, consumer staples, information technology, energy, financials, health care, industrials, materials, real estate, communication services, and utilities.

Industry:

Industries, or sub-sectors, are categories within the primary sectors which produce products in relation to their primary sector. For example, Apple creates products pertaining to the information technology sector, putting it in the technology hardware sub-sector/industry.

Dividend Yield:

The dividend yield which a security provides, is a percentage of the share you earn in return for holding said shares. For example, stock A has a dividend yield of 3%, one would recieve the 3% of that stock for just owning it, on top of any growth or decay.

Dividend Stock:

Dividend Stocks are often described as securities who have little growth but pay high dividends in order to attract investors as they have already achieved a high enough market share in their industry, and have limited room for expansion or have slow expansion in their industry.

Cyclical Stock

Cyclical stocks are securites in which their performance is largely based upon the macroeconomic situation. If the economy does well, they typicall outperform the rest, if they economy has a downturn, they typicall underperform. A prime example would airline stocks, who thrived for periods of travel, but crashed the hardest during the beginning of the pandemic in 2020.

Defensive Stock:

Defensive stocks are companies that typically underperform in a booming economy, but are little changed in an era of hard economic downturn. They most often provide dividends and a stable price without consideration for anything outside of their own operations.

Growth Stock:

Growth stocks are characterized as companies who are expected to outperform the broader market in any period of time.

Value Stock:

Value stocks are securities with share prices typically below their fundamental value, often due to low interest in industry and lower volume. Their price is typically swung by a few wealthy investors, earning, revenue, and dividen payments.

Elliott Wave Theory:

Elliott Wave Theory is the idea that markets move in a five wave structure with impulsive and corrective waves. Impulsive waves are ones which follow the security's general trend with five waves within them, being fractal. Correct waves on the other hand, are waves which go the opposite direction of the trend, and have a three wave structure within them. After a five wive sequence is completed, an a-b-c event occurs.
The process occurs as follows (assume bull case):

  1. Wave 1: An impulsive wave up
  2. Wave 2: A corrective wave down, but does not fall below wave 1's low
  3. Wave 3: Another impulsive wave up, typically the largest growth occurs here
  4. Wave 4: A corrective wave down, cannot break the low of wave 2
  5. Wave 5: A final impulsive wave up, breaking the peak of wave 3, calling for the end of the cycle, initating the a-b-c process.
  6. A: the security is driven downwards
  7. B: the security is driven upwards
  8. C: the security is driven further downwards than wave a, finishing the structure and awaits a new trend to be established for the process to repeat.

Fibonacci Extensions:

Fibonacci extensions are a specific set of numbers in the Fibonacci Sequence typically observed in the market. These extensions are used to set price targets for both times of growth and pullback, possibly giving a clue to where the next price target will be. The numbers most often seen in the market are the 61.8%, 100%, 200%, and 261.8% extensions. However, recent unprecedented growth has introducted the 423.6% extension to be observed more often.

Volume:

The amount of shares exchanged in a given amount of time.

Relative Strength Index:

The Relative Strength Index, or RSI, is an indicator of investor momentum in either the bullish or bearish direction. The security is considered overbought above 70%, and oversold when below 30%.
